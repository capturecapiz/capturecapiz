Brief History of the Province of Capiz

Capiz is unique interplay of history, myths and seafoods. Interestingly, through nobody is certain of how Capiz got its name, one legend narrates that the name of the province is in honor of one of the twin granddaughters of Dau Bankaya, the leader of the district comprising of what is today Capiz and Aklan. He divided his territories into two: Kapid and Akean. The Spandiards who later settled in the area adopted the names of Capiz and Aklan. Another legend says that it was Datu Bangkaya's wife who gave birth to the twin daughters sometimes in 1570. The term "twin" in local Capisnon language is "kapid".

The significances of seafood in the lives of early inhabitants was clearly reflected in other versions. When Spaniards came to Capiz, they saw its water abundant with certain type of fish. When they asked the name of fish, the natives answered "Lapis". It is claimed that the conquerors heard "kapis" and so the place was named. The most popular version is that when the Spaniards settled in Capiz, they found an abundance of mullusk known locally as "PIOS" or "KAPIS". The shell "kapis" is utilized for windows and decorative items.

In precolonial times, Panay Island was called Sinugbohan. Capiz was part of Akean (now Aklan) anhd its name was Ilaya, thus its people were called Ilayahon or Ilayanon.

In 1569, Miguel Lopez de Legaspi decided to leave Cebu to settle in Bamban (the present town of Panay) - the second Spanish settlement in the country established in 1567 by his nephew, Felipe de Salcedo. One of the two main reasons for this os seeing the advantage of the place following the reporty of one of his captains: "pan hay in esta isla" (there is food on the island).

In August 27, 1570, Legaspi created the province of Panay. In 1572, he founded the province of Ogtong (Oton) in the southern part of the island, and to it, he annexed Akean. He placed Panai under the administrative jurisdiction of Ogtong.

Sometimes in the 18th century, Panai was renamed Capiz and the name Panai (later spelled PANAY) was givin to the island. When the Spaniards reorganized the island in 1703, they made Akean a district of Capiz. On April 15, 1901, the United States Philippine Commission through Act No. 115 established the second civil government of Capiz. Aklan which was then part of Capiz was seperated on April 25, 1956 through Republic Act 1414 signed by President Ramon Magsaysay.

Today, the people of Capiz are called Capisnon (ca-Pis-non). They predominantly belong to the brown race. Initially, they were a blend of the indigene, the Negrito, the Indonesian and the Malay. later, the Arab, Chinese, American and other races modified the blend.

Capiz is a home of two Inigenous Peoples Communities, Panay Bukidnon and Ati, who had preserved their culture by faithfully passing it on from generation to generation. Their philosophy, values, traditions, rituals, language, games, foods, architechture, literature, music, dance, embroidery express a unique way of life of respect and love, and interconnectedness of the Divine, fellowman and environment.

Unmatched taste of seafood, beautiful and clen environment, and inspiring cultural experience are impetus combined that molded Capisnons to be highly-oriented in community development and artistically inclined.
